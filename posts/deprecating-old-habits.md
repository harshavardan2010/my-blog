Every system accumulates dead code.

Routines that made sense at a particular point in time, then stayed running long after the reason for them disappeared. The 9pm scroll. The checking of messages before coffee. The reflexive tab-opening that leads nowhere productive.

These aren't bad habits in any dramatic sense. They're just processes that are consuming cycles without producing output.

## Running the Audit

The first step is noticing what you actually do, not what you think you do.

For one week, I kept a rough log. Not productivity tracking in the aggressive sense — just noting, after the fact, what I had actually spent time on. The gaps between what I intended and what I did were instructive.

The intended: deep work, reading, deliberate rest.

The actual: a lot of time in the shallow end of the information pool.

## Garbage Collection

In programming, garbage collection is the process of freeing up memory that's no longer being used. The program doesn't delete things you're still working with — it identifies what has no live references and reclaims the space.

The personal equivalent is asking: *what am I doing that has no live connection to anything I actually care about?*

Not everything needs to be optimized. Rest is not dead code. Aimless reading can be generative. The point is to tell the difference between genuine rest and the kind of low-grade stimulation that feels like rest but leaves you more tired.

## Reallocating

The freed-up time didn't automatically fill with better things. That part required deliberate redirection.

One rule that helped: before opening a browser or a feed, name the thing you're looking for. If you can't name it, you don't need it yet.

Simple. Mostly effective. Still running.
