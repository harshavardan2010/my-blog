There is something deeply satisfying about a design that knows what it cannot do.

The Game Boy's screen was green and muddy. Its resolution was barely enough to render a face. And yet the games made for it are remembered with a kind of fierce affection that high-definition successors rarely inspire.

Constraints are not obstacles to creativity. They are the **shape** of it.

## The Infinite Canvas Problem

When you can do anything, the first question — what should I actually do? — becomes paralyzing. The blank white canvas of modern design tools is a kind of cruelty. Every pixel is negotiable. Every color is available. Every font can be licensed.

This freedom produces a certain aesthetic: smooth, legible, forgettable.

## What a Fixed Grid Teaches You

Working within a grid forces prioritization. If you only have 6 columns, every element must earn its place. You stop asking "where should this go?" and start asking "does this deserve to exist at all?"

That's a much better question.

## Remembering Forward

The interfaces we remember are usually ones that had no choice but to be distinctive. The boxy icons of early Mac OS. The dithered gradients of Windows 3.1. The bright plasticity of early web design with its tiled backgrounds and beveled buttons.

These weren't beautiful by accident. They were beautiful because their constraints forced decisions that infinite-choice design never would have made.

**Constraint breeds character.** The limitation is not a bug in the creative process. It is the process.
